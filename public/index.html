The Night I Paid Money to Sleep in a Crime Scene

A hostel in Phnom Penh so bad it made me reconsider sleeping outdoors. In Cambodia. During monsoon season.

Phnom Penh, Cambodia8 min read

Four dollars a night should have been the warning. I knew that. You know that. Everyone with a functioning prefrontal cortex knows that a bed advertised at four US dollars in central Phnom Penh is not a bed you want to sleep in. But I'd just come off a twelve-hour bus from Siem Reap where the air conditioning had been broken since roughly 2014, and the tuk-tuk driver who picked me up at the station said he knew a place. "Very clean, my friend. Very good." He said this with the confidence of a man who had never set foot inside.

The hostel had one of those names that tries way too hard. Three positive words crammed together, none of which applied. You know the type. I won't name it because I'm not trying to get sued, but just imagine the most aggressively optimistic name possible for a building that should probably be condemned.

I should describe the entrance, because "entrance" is doing a lot of heavy lifting here. You walked through a doorway that was partially blocked by a motorbike, then past a woman frying something in a wok balanced on a propane tank, then up a staircase so narrow my backpack scraped both walls simultaneously. The reception desk was a plastic chair with a laminated price list taped to it. Nobody was sitting in the chair. I stood there for about six minutes before a guy in boxer shorts wandered out of a room, took my four dollars, handed me a key attached to a wooden spoon, and wandered back without saying a word.

The Room That Technically Qualified as a Room

Room 7. Top floor, which in this building meant third floor, which in reality meant about two and a half because the ceilings were so low I could press my palms flat against them. The door opened inward and immediately hit the bed frame, so you had to sort of limbo sideways to get in. There was a fan on the ceiling that looked like it hadn't spun since the Clinton administration. I flicked the switch. Nothing. I flicked it again. A grinding noise, like a cat being slowly fed into a pencil sharpener, and then one blade started rotating while the other two stayed still.

The mattress had a history. I don't mean that in the interesting way, like "this mattress has stories to tell." I mean it in the forensic way, like someone should be dusting it for prints. It was stained in a pattern that suggested either a very large coffee had been spilled or something significantly worse had happened. The sheet was fitted in the sense that someone had tried to fit it and given up about 60% of the way through, leaving one corner of bare mattress exposed like a wound.

I sat on it anyway. I was tired. I was stupid.

A Bathroom That Should Be Reported to The Hague

The bathroom situation requires its own section because it was, and I mean this literally, the worst room I have ever stood in. The shower was a rubber hose duct-taped to the wall at about chest height. The toilet had no seat. Not "the seat was broken" but rather it had clearly never had one, like whoever installed it just thought the porcelain rim was fine for human sitting. The floor was permanently wet, not from recent use but from what seemed to be a fundamental architectural disagreement between the pipes and gravity. There was a smell. I won't describe the smell because some things should die with me, but I will say I brushed my teeth in the hallway with bottled water from that point forward.

The 2 AM Durian Incident

And then I met my roommates.

The hostel had apparently put me in an eight-bed dorm despite the listing saying "private room," a discrepancy that four-dollar-a-night establishments tend not to worry about. Three of the beds were occupied. Bunk one held a German guy named Klaus who was six foot four and snored like a chainsaw being drowned in a bathtub. Bunk two held a couple from somewhere in Eastern Europe who spent the entire evening arguing in hushed, venomous whispers that occasionally erupted into what sounded like threats. Bunk three was empty when I arrived but was claimed around 2 AM by a man who turned on the overhead fluorescent light, ate an entire durian fruit on his bed, and then fell asleep with the light still on.

The durian. In an enclosed room. With no ventilation. I want you to sit with that for a moment.

These Bedbugs Had Grandchildren

But the bedbugs were the real stars of the evening. I woke up around 4 AM with my left arm looking like a connect-the-dots puzzle. Little red welts in a neat line running from my wrist to my elbow, because bedbugs are nothing if not methodical. If you've never dealt with these little bastards, World Nomads has a solid guide on what to look for. I pulled back the sheet and saw them, actual living insects, just hanging out on the mattress like they'd been there longer than I had and frankly had more right to the bed. One of them was so fat and blood-drunk it barely moved when I flicked it. My blood. That was my blood inside that bug.

I got up, turned on my phone flashlight, and inspected the bed frame. The wooden slats had clusters of them in the joints, little rust-colored colonies tucked into every crack. This wasn't an infestation that had just started. This was generational. These bedbugs had grandchildren.

I spent the rest of the night sitting upright in the plastic chair in the hallway, using my backpack as a pillow and my sarong as a blanket, while stray cats wandered past and a rooster on a nearby rooftop started crowing at 4:45 AM because roosters in Southeast Asia have absolutely no respect for appropriate crowing hours.

Bug Spray for Half the Room Rate

At 6 AM I went downstairs to complain. The same guy in boxer shorts was there, now also wearing a tank top, which I suppose counted as getting dressed for work. I showed him the bites on my arm. I showed him a photo of the bugs on my phone. He looked at both, shrugged, and said "no refund." Then he offered to sell me a can of bug spray for two dollars, which, if you're keeping track, was half the room rate. I declined. He shrugged again. I got the strong sense he'd had this conversation before.

I walked out at 6:15 AM, covered in bites, smelling faintly of durian that had permeated my clothes, and booked a guesthouse down the road on Booking.com for eleven dollars a night that had actual sheets and a toilet seat and no visible wildlife. Eleven dollars. That's what separating myself from the bedbugs cost. Less than two pints back home.

The Toilet Seat Revelation

The thing is, I could have just paid the eleven dollars in the first place. I could have read the reviews, which I later checked and which included phrases like "do not stay here under any circumstances" and "I think someone died in my room." But budget travel does something to your brain. It rewires you. You start thinking four dollars is a deal instead of a warning. You start believing that discomfort is authenticity, that suffering is what separates real travellers from tourists. You tell yourself you're not the kind of person who needs a toilet seat.

You are. Everyone is. That's what toilet seats are for.

I still have a scar on my left forearm from where I scratched the bites too hard in the heat, a tiny constellation of pale dots that I'll carry with me longer than any souvenir I've ever bought. Some nights, when I'm falling asleep in a perfectly decent bed in a perfectly decent room, I think about that mattress in room 7 and the fat bedbug that wouldn't move, and I feel an itch that isn't really there. Four dollars a night, and I'm still paying for it.

Transport Nightmares

Nineteen Hours, One Chicken, Zero Sleep

The La Paz to Uyuni overnight bus. A journey that technically qualifies as transportation.

Bolivia9 min read

The Ticket Said "12 Hours." The Ticket Lied.

The bus from La Paz to Uyuni is supposed to take ten hours. I want you to understand that no human being has ever completed this journey in ten hours. Twelve is optimistic. Fourteen is realistic. Mine took nineteen, and by the end I had aged roughly forty years and developed a personal relationship with a chicken named, as far as I could tell, Roberto.

I bought the ticket at the La Paz bus terminal, which is itself a place that could break a person. Forty-odd competing ticket booths, all staffed by people screaming departure times at you like auctioneers having a breakdown. I picked the company with the newest-looking booth, which in retrospect is like choosing your dentist based on their curtains. The ticket cost 120 bolivianos, about twelve bucks. I thought that was a bargain. I was wrong. It was a ransom paid in advance for suffering not yet endured.

Boarding at the Hour of Regret

Departure was listed for 8pm. The bus arrived at 9:45. Nobody around me reacted to this, which should have been my first clue that time itself operates differently on Bolivian overnight routes. The bus looked like it had been manufactured during a period of history I'd associate more with the steam engine than modern road transport. It was painted in a colour I'd describe as "optimistic blue," faded to a defeated grey along the panels where the Altiplano winds had sandblasted the paint into memory.

I found my seat. Seat 23. The number was there, scratched into the metal frame, but what sat above that number was less a seat and more a suggestion. The recline function consisted of the backrest being permanently broken at a 110-degree angle, which sounds almost comfortable until you realise that the seat in front of you is broken at the same angle, directly into your kneecaps. I am five foot ten. I am not a large man. I was folded like a letter nobody wanted to send.

The Temperature Situation

The Altiplano at night drops to around minus ten. I knew this. I had a jumper, a coat, a scarf. What I did not account for was that the heating system on this bus appeared to work on a binary. It was either off completely, letting the Andean cold creep through the single-pane windows like something that wanted to kill you, or it was blasting what I can only describe as the exhalation of a dying furnace directly onto my ankles at roughly 400 degrees. There was no middle setting. My feet were being slow-roasted while my face developed frostbite. I was simultaneously sweating and shivering, which I didn't think the human body could do until that night proved me comprehensively wrong.

The Driver and His Relationship with Mortality

Somewhere around midnight we began the descent off the plateau and onto the unpaved roads that connect the Altiplano to the salt flats. I use the word "roads" with tremendous generosity. These were suggestions of paths, carved into dust and rock, with drops on one side that I couldn't see the bottom of in the dark but could absolutely feel in my stomach every time the driver took a corner at a speed that implied he had made peace with God sometime earlier that evening.

He honked constantly. Not at other vehicles, because there were no other vehicles. Just into the void. Into the black Bolivian night. Honking, I think, at the concept of danger itself, as if the horn was a ward against physics. Every ten or fifteen minutes the bus would hit a pothole so deep that the entire vehicle would leave the ground for a moment, and forty-odd passengers would levitate briefly in their broken seats before crashing back down in a chorus of groans and swearing in three languages.

The woman next to me slept through all of this. I still think about her. What had she survived in her life that this bus was restful by comparison?

Roberto and the 3am Stop

At roughly 3am the bus stopped in what appeared to be nowhere. Not a town. Not a village. A building, alone, made of concrete blocks, with a single fluorescent tube buzzing above a door. Everyone got off. I got off. Inside was a woman selling soup and bread for 10 bolivianos, and the soup was genuinely one of the best things I have ever eaten in my life, which tells you either that the soup was extraordinary or that suffering is the greatest seasoning known to humanity. Probably both.

It was during this stop that Roberto the chicken made his entrance. A man boarding at this middle-of-nowhere waypoint carried a woven bag that moved. He sat two rows ahead of me and placed the bag on the floor, where it continued to move and occasionally cluck. Nobody said anything. The driver glanced at the bag and nodded, like a bouncer approving someone's outfit. Roberto spent the remaining hours of the journey providing a low, persistent soundtrack of displeasure that honestly mirrored my own internal state so perfectly I began to feel a bond with him.

Dawn, Dust, and the Concept of Arrival

The sun came up over the Altiplano at around 6:30, and I will grudgingly admit it was one of the most spectacular things I've ever seen. Pink and orange light spilling across an endless flat white landscape, the salt crust glowing like the surface of another planet. My back was destroyed. My neck had fused into a single angry knot. I hadn't slept. But for about four minutes, looking out that scratched, filthy window, I forgot all of it.

Then we hit another pothole and my head bounced off the glass and the moment was over.

We pulled into Uyuni at 3pm the following day. Nineteen hours. The town itself is a dusty, wind-blasted grid of concrete buildings that exists purely as a staging point for salt flat tours, and arriving there felt less like reaching a destination and more like being released from captivity. I stepped off the bus on legs that had forgotten their purpose. Roberto's owner walked past me carrying the woven bag, still clucking, and I swear the chicken looked more rested than I did.

I found a hostel. 40 bolivianos for a bed. The mattress was a foam pad roughly two inches thick laid on plywood. It was, without exaggeration, the most comfortable thing I had ever felt. I slept for eleven hours. I dreamt of nothing, which was the greatest luxury Bolivia had offered me so far.

Would I Do It Again

People ask me this and I always say no, absolutely not, never. But there's a version of the truth I don't usually share, which is that some part of me, some broken and probably masochistic part, is glad I did it. Not because it was fun. It was categorically not fun. But because every comfortable journey I've taken since then has felt like an unearned gift. I sat on a train from London to Edinburgh last year, smooth rails, a table, a plug socket, a man selling coffee from a cart, and I nearly cried with gratitude. That bus from La Paz broke something in me, and whatever it broke needed breaking.

Roberto, wherever you are, I hope you're free.

Food Poisoning Diaries

The Lamb Tagine That Loved Me Back

Eleven hours in Marrakech before my intestines declared war on the rest of my body.

Marrakech, Morocco8 min read

I'd been in Marrakech for exactly eleven hours before my intestines declared war on the rest of my body. Eleven hours. That's not even enough time to get jet lag sorted, but apparently it's plenty of time to ingest something that would rearrange my entire digestive system for the better part of a week.

Let me set the scene without setting the scene. It's Jemaa el-Fnaa at around nine in the evening, the smoke from about forty different grills is hanging in the air like fog, and I'm standing in front of a stall watching a man ladle something that looks and smells like the best thing I've ever encountered. Lamb tagine with preserved lemon and olives. Thick, glistening, the kind of thing food photographers dream about. 30 dirhams. About two bucks fifty. I handed over the money like I was doing the smartest thing anyone had ever done.

Reader, it was not the smartest thing anyone had ever done.

The Golden Hours (Before Everything Went Wrong)

I'll give that tagine this much. For about four hours, it was genuinely one of the best meals I'd eaten in months. The lamb fell apart on the fork. The sauce had that deep, slow-cooked warmth that you can taste in your chest. I walked back to my riad in the Mellah feeling like a man who had cracked the code of budget travel. Incredible food, pennies on the pound, stars overhead, the sound of the medina settling into its nighttime hum all around me.

I even texted my mate Dave. "You absolute mug sitting in the pub back home. I'm living the dream out here."

Dave would have the last laugh. Dave always has the last laugh.

2:47 AM, or, The Reckoning

I woke up and something was deeply, fundamentally wrong. You know that feeling where your stomach makes a noise that sounds less like digestion and more like a radiator about to explode? That. My entire midsection was doing something I can only describe as hostile negotiations with reality. I lay there for maybe thirty seconds telling myself it was fine, just a bit of indigestion, travel belly, nothing serious.

Then I moved.

Moving was a mistake. Moving activated whatever biological countdown had been silently ticking away, and suddenly I was sprinting across a dark room, crashing into a suitcase, bouncing off a wall, and arriving at a toilet that I would not leave for the next ninety minutes. I will spare you the forensic details but I will say this. I didn't know the human body could do that from both ends simultaneously. I genuinely thought that was a myth, something people exaggerated for comedic effect. It is not a myth. It is violently, humiliatingly real, and it is not comedic when you are the one experiencing it on a toilet in a 400-year-old riad at three in the morning while a cat screams on the roof.

The Bathroom Situation (A Horror Story in Its Own Right)

Now. The riad was beautiful. Hand-carved plaster, zellige tiles everywhere, a little courtyard with orange trees and a fountain. Gorgeous. What it was not was built for a six-foot man who needed to be within lunging distance of a bathroom at all times. My room was on the second floor. The nearest toilet was across the courtyard and up three steps. Three steps that might as well have been Everest when your body has decided that gravity is now your enemy.

The toilet itself was the traditional kind. A hole in the floor with foot plates. On a normal day, fine, no problem, when in Rome and all that. But when you're shaking and sweating and your legs have turned to something with the structural integrity of overcooked spaghetti, squatting over a hole in the floor becomes an athletic event you have not trained for. I pulled a muscle in my left thigh. During food poisoning. On a toilet. Try explaining that one to a physiotherapist back home.

Local Remedies, or, How to Make a Bad Situation Worse

By morning I was a ghost. The lovely woman who ran the riad took one look at me and started shaking her head in a way that suggested she'd seen this exact scene play out a hundred times before. She disappeared into the kitchen and came back with a glass of something that looked like muddy water and smelled like a garden centre had burned down.

"Cumin and lemon," she said. "Drink."

I drank. It tasted exactly how you'd expect cumin dissolved in warm lemon water to taste, which is to say like punishment. She also gave me a handful of dried mint leaves and told me to chew them. I chewed them. I felt nothing except minty and still extremely ill.

A taxi driver I stumbled past later that day, during a brave but ultimately foolish attempt to find a pharmacy, told me to drink Coca-Cola that had been left open overnight so the fizz went flat. When I pointed out that I didn't have Coca-Cola or the ability to wait overnight, he shrugged and said "Inshallah." Which is fair enough, really.

The pharmacy, when I finally found it in a side street off Avenue Mohammed V, sold me a box of something called Ercefuryl and a packet of oral rehydration salts that tasted like drinking the ocean if the ocean were also somehow chalky. The pharmacist held up two fingers. Two days, he said. Two days and I'd be fine. He was right, almost to the hour, which made me wonder just how often he has this exact conversation.

The Regret Inventory

During the worst of it, lying on my bed with a wet flannel on my forehead like a Victorian consumptive, I conducted a thorough inventory of my life choices. The tagine was the obvious villain, but was it really? I'd also eaten a mystery meat sandwich from a cart near Bab Doukkala around lunchtime. And some olives from a massive open-air barrel that roughly ten thousand flies had been treating as a swimming pool. And I'd definitely had a glass of orange juice from one of the stalls in the square, the ones where they squeeze the oranges right in front of you, which feels reassuring until you notice the glasses are being "washed" in a bucket of water that hasn't been changed since the Almoravid dynasty.

Could have been any of them. Could have been all of them working in concert like some kind of bacterial orchestra, each playing their own instrument of destruction. (If you think this is bad, read about the lassi in Varanasi that nearly finished me off.)

Day Three, or, The Fragile Peace

By Wednesday I could eat plain bread without my stomach making threats. By Thursday I managed half a bowl of harira soup and felt like I'd climbed a mountain. By Friday I was back in the medina, walking past the food stalls in Jemaa el-Fnaa, and here's the thing that nobody tells you about food poisoning abroad. You go right back. You walk past the exact same kind of stall, and the smoke hits you, and the smell of cumin and charcoal and saffron fills your head, and some part of your brain that clearly wasn't paying attention during the trauma goes "that looks nice actually."

I didn't eat the tagine again. I'm not completely stupid. I had a chicken bastilla from a different stall instead, all flaky pastry and cinnamon and icing sugar, and it was perfect, and nothing bad happened, and I walked back to the riad feeling like maybe the whole thing had been worth it after all.

It wasn't worth it. I lost four pounds and a muscle in my thigh. But the bastilla was genuinely excellent.

€40 for a Friendship Bracelet I Didn't Want

How Marrakech separated me from my money, my dignity, and briefly my phone.

Marrakech, Morocco9 min read

The One Where I Paid €40 for a Friendship Bracelet I Didn't Want

I'd been in Marrakech for exactly four hours when I lost all my street smarts. Four hours. That's how long it took for the Jemaa el-Fnaa to completely rewire my brain from "seasoned traveller" to "wide-eyed idiot holding a monkey."

But I'm getting ahead of myself. The monkey comes later.

A Nice Man Who Definitely Wasn't Leading Me Into a Trap

I was trying to find the tanneries. Had a vague sense they were somewhere north of the main square, which in the medina means absolutely nothing because north is a concept that ceases to exist once you pass through those sandstone archways. Every alley curves. Every turn looks the same. The smell of cedar and raw leather drifts from seventeen directions at once, and the ground beneath your shoes shifts between cobblestone, packed dirt, and what I really hope was just mud.

So when a guy around my age fell into step beside me and said "Tanneries? I'm going that way, my friend," I thought, great. Convenient. He wasn't pushy about it. Didn't grab my arm or anything. Just walked alongside me chatting about football, asking where I was from, telling me his cousin lived in Manchester. Classic stuff. I knew the playbook. I'd read the blogs. I was aware, on an intellectual level, that friendly strangers in tourist zones are usually working an angle.

But he was so genuinely likeable. That's the thing nobody warns you about properly. They're warm and funny and they make you feel like a bit of a racist for being suspicious.

So I followed him.

The Scenic Route Through Someone's Living Room

We took a turn I would never have found on my own, through a doorway so narrow I had to angle my shoulders, down a passage that smelled like mint tea and donkey, and suddenly we were on a rooftop overlooking the tannery pits. The famous ones. Hundreds of stone vats filled with dye in every colour, workers knee-deep in white liquid that I later learned was pigeon droppings mixed with quicklime. The whole thing hit my nose like a wall of hot garbage wrapped in ammonia. Someone pressed a sprig of fresh mint into my hand. I held it to my face and breathed through it like a gas mask while my eyes watered.

And the view was genuinely spectacular. I'll give them that. The leather pits of Marrakech look like a giant paint palette left out in the sun, these perfect circles of saffron yellow and poppy red and deep indigo, with tiny figures moving between them. Worth seeing. No question.

The problem was everything that happened next.

Would You Like to See Where the Magic Happens

My new best friend handed me off to another guy who worked in the leather shop attached to the terrace. Seamless transition. Like a relay race, except I was the baton and also the prize money. This second bloke gave me a tour of the workshop downstairs, showed me how they soften the hides, how they cut the leather, how they stitch the bags. He put a jacket on me. Then a different jacket. Then he started pulling bags off shelves and opening them in front of me while saying things like "feel that, feel the quality" and "for you, special price."

I didn't want a leather bag. I don't even carry a bag. I'm the kind of person who shoves their passport in their back pocket and calls it packing. But I was standing in this man's workshop, wearing his jacket, surrounded by his colleagues, and the social pressure was extraordinary. It was like being at a dinner party where the host cooked something terrible and everyone's pretending it's delicious. You just go along with it because the alternative is confrontation, and you're Australian, so you'd rather just cop it than cause a scene.

I bought a wallet for 350 dirham. About thirty-five bucks. The leather was decent, I'll admit. But I already had a wallet. I now owned two wallets, which is exactly zero wallets more than any person needs, and the guy who "happened to be walking my way" was waiting outside for his commission.

But Wait, There's a Monkey

Emboldened by having only been mildly ripped off, I wandered back to Jemaa el-Fnaa feeling like I'd handled things pretty well, actually. Thirty-five bucks for a wallet and a rooftop view of the tanneries. Cultural experience. Not bad. I was feeling good about myself, which is always the moment before disaster.

A man with a Barbary macaque on a chain made eye contact with me. I smiled. Never smile at a man with a monkey. That should be in every guidebook, printed in red, underlined. The monkey was on my shoulder before I could process what was happening. Another monkey appeared on my other shoulder. A third was placed in my hands. Someone put a snake around my neck. I was suddenly a human Christmas tree decorated exclusively with animals, and three guys were taking photos on MY phone, which one of them had gently removed from my hand.

Then they wanted 200 dirham per animal. Plus the snake.

I negotiated down to 100 dirham total, which felt like a victory at the time but was absolutely not a victory. I paid ten bucks to hold animals I didn't ask to hold while strangers took photos on my own phone that I could have taken myself if I'd had any control over the situation whatsoever.

The Moment of Clarity in a Mint Tea Shop

I sat down at a cafe on a side street afterward, ordered a pot of mint tea for 15 dirham, and did the maths on my afternoon. The wallet. The monkeys. The snake surcharge. The tip I gave my "friend" because I felt guilty. I was down about €55, and I'd been in the city less than a full day. At this rate I'd be bankrupt by Thursday.

The tea was perfect, though. Poured from a height into a small glass, searingly hot, sweet enough to make my teeth ache. The cafe had blue tile walls and a ceiling fan that wobbled with every rotation like it was about to launch itself into orbit. An old man at the next table was playing a card game I didn't recognise with two friends, slapping cards down with tremendous force and arguing about every hand.

I sat there for an hour, watching the light change on the alley walls from bright gold to deep orange, and I thought about what an absolute muppet I'd been. The thing is, I knew. I knew every step of the way that I was being played. The friendly guide, the rooftop, the leather shop, the incremental commitment. I'd read about all of it. I just thought it wouldn't work on me, because I thought I was clever, which is exactly the kind of person it works on best.

Lessons I Definitely Won't Remember Next Time

The wallet fell apart within three months. The stitching came undone first, then the dye started transferring onto everything it touched, and eventually the whole thing sort of dissolved like it was made of compressed tea leaves. I kept it anyway, stuffed in a drawer somewhere, because throwing it away would mean admitting the full extent of my stupidity out loud, and I wasn't ready for that conversation with myself.

The worst part of reef rash is trying to sleep in a cheap guesthouse where the sheets have the thread count of sandpaper and the humidity is running at about nine hundred percent.

You lie on your good side. Fine for twenty minutes. Then you shift. The sheet peels off your wound like a wax strip and you're suddenly wide awake, staring at the ceiling fan, questioning every decision that led you to this bed in this room in this country. You try sleeping on your back. The sheet sticks again. You try sleeping with no sheet. The mosquitoes find you in under a minute. You try wrapping your leg in a sarong. It sticks worse than the sheet.

I spent three nights barely sleeping, peeling fabric off my leg every time I moved, applying Betadine in the dark like some kind of midnight nurse, and lying there listening to the geckos on the wall wondering if this is what surf magazines mean by "the trip of a lifetime."

Would I Do It Again

I went back out two days later because I am not a smart man. Caught two waves on the inside, both tiny, both forgettable. Came in voluntarily before the reef could finish what it started. Sat in the warung with another Bintang and watched the guys who actually knew what they were doing thread through sections that would have sent me to a hospital in Denpasar.

I've still got a scar on my left shin that goes white when I get cold. My mates reckon it looks tough. I tell them it was bigger than it was, because that's what you do. But late at night, when I'm lying in a proper bed with proper sheets that don't stick to anything, I can still feel that reef under my skin, reminding me of the day the Indian Ocean looked me dead in the eye and said mate, you are not ready for this.

Surf Trips Gone Wrong

Outperformed by a Ten-Year-Old on a SpongeBob Board

The board snapped. The next one had no fins. I ended up bodyboarding.

Weligama, Sri Lanka6 min read

The Board That Hope Built

I've made some questionable decisions in my life but handing over 1,500 rupees for a surfboard held together with duct tape and what I can only assume was spiritual conviction ranks pretty high. The guy at the rental shack picked it up like he was presenting a newborn. "Very strong board, brother. No problem." There was a crack running down the middle that you could fit a coin into. The nose had been reattached at some point using what looked like bathroom sealant. I took it anyway because the next nearest rental place was apparently "just down the road" which in Sri Lankan terms means somewhere between twenty minutes and next Tuesday.

Two Waves and a Funeral

I paddled out feeling optimistic, which should have been my first warning sign. The first wave was fine. A gentle little thing, barely worth standing up for, but I stood up anyway and felt briefly like a person who knew what they were doing. Wave number two was slightly bigger. Nothing scary. Maybe chest high on a short day. I popped up, started trimming along the face, heard a sound like someone snapping a cricket bat over their knee, and then I was underwater holding two separate pieces of surfboard.

I surfaced to find the rental guy already waving from the beach. Not waving with concern. Waving like he wanted to have a chat about payment for damages. Mate, the thing was already damaged when I got it. That's like buying a car with no engine and getting billed when it won't start.

He had this way of looking disappointed in me that reminded me of my dad and I just handed over the money.

We had a fifteen-minute negotiation that ended with me paying another 800 bucks for the privilege of having destroyed something that was already destroyed. I'm not proud of how quickly I caved but he had this way of looking disappointed in me that reminded me of my dad and I just handed over the money.

Board Number Two, or "The Finless Wonder"

Further down the beach there was another place renting boards. This one looked more legit. The boards were standing upright in a rack instead of lying in the sand next to a sleeping dog. Progress. I picked out a decent-looking 7'2 and carried it to the water before noticing something was off. Flipped it over. No fins. Not like the fins were broken. The fin boxes were there but completely empty, just three little rectangular holes staring up at me like the board was as surprised as I was.

I walked back. "This one has no fins." The guy looked at it, looked at me, nodded slowly, and said "Yes." That was it. Yes. Like I'd pointed out that the sky was blue. He didn't offer a solution or a different board. Just confirmed the observation and went back to his phone.

I tried two more boards from his rack. One had fins but was so waterlogged it weighed about as much as a small fridge. The other was a shortboard designed for someone who weighs sixty kilos and actually knows how to surf, so that ruled me out on both counts.

The Great Equipment Hunt of Weligama

What followed was an hour-long march through town asking everyone if they knew where I could rent a surfboard that functioned as a surfboard. A tuk-tuk driver offered to take me to his cousin's place. A woman at a grocery shop pointed vaguely toward the south. A bloke at a guesthouse said he had one I could borrow but when he brought it out it was a windsurf board from what appeared to be 1987, complete with a mast hole in the middle. I stared at it for a while. He stared at me staring at it. Neither of us said anything.

The heat was getting unbearable and I'd sweated through my rashie twice over. I sat on the seawall, drank a King Coconut that cost me 50 bucks, and watched the ocean mock me with perfect little peeling waves that were going completely unridden.

Humbled by a Ten-Year-Old

That's when I noticed the kid. Couldn't have been older than ten. Skinny little legend on a foam bodyboard, absolutely tearing the place apart. He was launching off sections, pulling into closeouts on purpose just for laughs, and paddling back out faster than I could on a good day with flippers. I watched him for a solid twenty minutes with a mixture of admiration and deep personal shame.

During a lull he paddled in and sat near me on the sand. We got talking. His English was way better than my Sinhala, which consists entirely of the word "thank you" pronounced badly. I told him I couldn't find a board. He looked at me with genuine pity, the kind of pity a surgeon gives you before delivering bad news, and said "You can use mine."

So that's how I ended up bodyboarding in Sri Lanka. Thirty-one years old, lying on a bright yellow foam board that had SpongeBob on it, getting repeatedly pummelled by waves that this child had been styling on minutes earlier. Every time I caught one he'd cheer from the beach. Encouraging. Supportive. The way you cheer for a dog that finally learns to shake hands.

Lessons from the Foam

I gave the board back after an hour, bought the kid an ice cream that cost less than a single fin plug, and walked back to my guesthouse smelling like reef and failure. My shoulders were sunburned in the shape of the rashie I'd taken off because it was too hot, which meant I looked like a human neapolitan ice cream for the rest of the week.

Somewhere in that town there are still surfboards with no fins and surfboards held together with tape and surfboards that are technically windsurfers. And somewhere on that beach there's a ten-year-old kid on a SpongeBob bodyboard who surfs better than I ever will. I think about him sometimes, usually when I'm buying expensive gear I don't deserve, and I reckon he's still out there, ripping, not needing any of it.

Transport Nightmares

The Overnight Train That Smelled Like Defeat

Bangkok to Chiang Mai. Twelve hours of broken AC, stranger's feet, and a mysterious wet patch.

Thailand7 min read

They Said It Would Be an Adventure

Budget travel guides make overnight trains sound romantic. The gentle sway, the clickety-clack, waking up in a new country with the sunrise. What they don't mention is the bloke on the bunk above you who smells like he marinates in fish sauce, or the air conditioning unit that gave up sometime during the Khmer Rouge era.

I booked the sleeper from Bangkok to Chiang Mai because I'm apparently still capable of poor decisions after six months on the road. Saved me 80 bucks compared to flying. That's four nights in a hostel, or roughly 160 Pad Thais if you're keeping score. The math made sense at the time.

The Bunk Bed From Hell

My bunk was upper level, which the cheerful woman at the ticket counter assured me was "very good, very comfortable." She lied with the confidence of someone who takes domestic flights. The mattress was about as thick as a pancake and roughly as supportive. I've slept on beach towels with more cushioning.

Below me was a middle-aged Thai businessman who immediately kicked off his shoes and unleashed a smell that could strip paint. Above me, nothing but a ceiling so close I could lick it if I were into that sort of thing. The whole setup felt like a very slow-moving coffin.

The AC was broken. Not partially broken, not struggling, but completely and utterly deceased. Outside it was 34 degrees and humid enough to grow moss. Inside it was somehow hotter. Everyone in the carriage looked like they'd just finished a marathon through a sauna.

Meet Your New Best Mate

Two hours in, the guy across the aisle decided we were friends. I was staring out the window trying to pretend I was literally anywhere else when he leaned over and asked where I was from. I made the mistake of answering.

Turns out he was heading to Chiang Mai for a wedding and had already started celebrating with a bottle of Sang Som that smelled like it could power a motorbike. He wanted to share. He wanted to talk about Australian football. He wanted to show me 47 photos of his niece. I wanted to melt into the floor.

At some point he passed out with his foot dangling off his bunk and into my personal space. I spent the next three hours with a stranger's toes approximately six inches from my face. They had their own smell. It wasn't good.

The Mysterious Wet Patch Incident

Around 2am I woke up to something wet on my arm. My first thought was that the ceiling was leaking, which seemed plausible given the general state of disrepair. Then I realized the wetness was warm. I don't want to know what it was. I've chosen to believe it was condensation and I'm sticking to that story.

I wiped it off with my shirt and tried to go back to sleep, which is when I discovered that sleeping on a train is actually impossible. Every time I'd start to drift off, we'd hit a curve and I'd roll into the wall. Or we'd hit the brakes and I'd nearly launch myself into the aisle. The rhythm was less "gentle sway" and more "aggressive tumble dry cycle."

By 4am I'd given up entirely. I climbed down and sat in the corridor with two other defeated souls, a German backpacker and a British woman who looked like she was reconsidering every choice that led her to this moment. We didn't talk much. What was there to say.

The Arrival That Wasn't

We were supposed to arrive at 6am. By 8am we were still rolling through farmland with no city in sight. By 10am people were getting genuinely annoyed. The conductor walked through occasionally looking harassed but offering no explanation. Someone said there'd been a delay because of track maintenance. Someone else said it was a broken coupling on another train ahead of us. I suspect they just forgot where they were going.

We finally pulled into Chiang Mai station at noon, a full six hours late. I stumbled off the train looking like I'd been in a fight with a tumble dryer and lost. My back hurt. My neck hurt. I smelled like a combination of fish sauce guy, Sang Som vapors, and defeat.

The first thing I did was find a guesthouse with working air conditioning. The second thing was to book a flight back to Bangkok, because there was absolutely no chance I was getting on that train again. Worth the extra 80 bucks just to maintain my sanity and avoid any more surprise foot-to-face encounters.

The Takeaway I Didn't Want

The whole experience reminded me why I'd sworn off sleeper trains after that disaster hostel in Cambodia with the bedbugs. Sometimes the budget option costs you in ways that don't show up on the balance sheet. Like your dignity, your spine alignment, and your faith in public transport.

If you're going to do overnight trains in Southeast Asia anyway, at least get proper travel insurance so when you arrive six hours late and miss your connection you're not completely screwed. And maybe pack some earplugs, a face mask, and a hazmat suit.

I'm writing this from a cafe in Chiang Mai with excellent wifi and air conditioning that actually works. My back still hurts. I can still smell fish sauce guy in my nightmares. But I saved 80 bucks, so there's that.

Hostel Horrors

The Hostel That Was Definitely Someone's Apartment

A fourth-floor walkup in Budapest. Bunk beds in his bedroom. His cat on my pillow.

Budapest, Hungary7 min read

The Listing Said Hostel. The Reality Said Someone Lives Here.

There's a special category of budget accommodation that exists in Eastern Europe. Not quite a hostel, not quite a hotel, definitely not legal. I'm talking about the apartment-hostel hybrid where some entrepreneurial local has shoved bunk beds into their spare bedroom and listed it on Booking.com for seventeen bucks a night.

I found mine in Budapest. The photos showed a bright, modern space with exposed brick and tasteful minimalist furniture. What they didn't show was the bloke who owned the place sleeping on the couch in the living room I had to walk through to get to the bathroom at 3am.

Red Flag Number One Should Have Been Obvious

The address wasn't a street number. It was an apartment building with a buzzer system and a code the host texted me twenty minutes before I arrived. No sign, no reception, just a fourth-floor walkup in a residential block where someone's grandmother was definitely watching me haul my pack up the stairs.

When I knocked, a guy in tracksuit pants and a ratty Adidas shirt answered. Not staff. The owner. He looked like I'd just woken him up, which was concerning given it was 2pm on a Tuesday.

He showed me to my "dorm". It was his bedroom with three bunk beds crammed in where a normal person would have, I don't know, a bed and a dresser. My bunk had sheets that smelled like fabric softener and optimism. The mattress had the structural integrity of a pool float.

The Bathroom Situation Was Exactly What You're Imagining

We shared a bathroom. Me, five other backpackers who looked equally confused about where they'd ended up, and the owner. His toothbrush sat in a cup next to the sink. His shampoo bottles lined the shower ledge. A half-used tube of athlete's foot cream occupied the counter like a tiny flag of defeat.

There was one towel hook. Six guests. You do the math.

The toilet seat was covered in one of those fuzzy covers that your nan might have in 1987. Every time I sat down I wondered how many strangers' arses had compressed those same synthetic fibers. Then I'd try very hard to stop wondering.

Breakfast Was A Generous Term For What Happened

The listing promised breakfast included. What that meant in practice was access to whatever the owner had in his fridge. Two-day-old bread, a jar of Nutella with knife marks scraped through it, and some suspicious yogurt that may have predated my arrival by several weeks.

One morning I watched him eat cereal in his underwear while scrolling through his phone. We made eye contact. Neither of us acknowledged it. I ate my sad Nutella toast standing up.

The kitchen had exactly two forks, one pot, and a frying pan with a handle held on by electrical tape. This was not a space designed for guests. This was a space designed for a single man to heat up frozen pizza and pretend vegetables don't exist.

The Cat Knew This Was Weird Too

Did I mention the cat? There was a cat. A large orange tabby that had the run of the place and seemed deeply unimpressed by the rotating cast of confused travelers invading his territory.

He'd sleep on my bunk when I wasn't there, leaving behind a layer of fur and judgment. One night he knocked over my travel toiletries at 4am in what I can only assume was a deliberate act of protest.

The owner called him something unpronounceable and seemed genuinely baffled when I asked if maybe the cat shouldn't be in the dorm room where people were trying to sleep. The cat stayed. We learned to coexist.

The Other Guests Were In On The Confusion

Nobody talked about it directly, but we all knew. This wasn't a hostel. This was some guy's apartment that he'd monetized with the minimum possible effort. We'd pass each other in the hallway with the awkward energy of people who'd all made the same mistake and were too cheap to leave.

One girl from Germany tried to be diplomatic about it. "It's very local," she said. By which she meant it was absolutely mental and she'd already booked somewhere else for her next city.

A Canadian guy tried to ask the owner about the lack of common space and got a shrug and something about the living room being available "if you want". The living room where the owner slept on a pull-out couch surrounded by his personal belongings and a TV playing Hungarian game shows at volumes designed to assert dominance.

Looking Back With The Clarity Distance Provides

I stayed four nights. Not because it was good, but because I'd already paid and my trip budget was being held together with wishful thinking and travel insurance I hoped I'd never need to use. Every morning I'd wake up to cat fur and the smell of someone else's breakfast. Every night I'd climb into my saggy bunk and wonder how this was legal.

It wasn't the worst place I've stayed. That honor belongs to a guesthouse in Bangkok where I'm pretty sure organized crime was happening in the next room. But it was definitely the most surreal. The strange limbo of being a paying guest in someone's actual home while they lived around you like you were the imposition.

The owner left me a good review. Said I was quiet and respectful. I left him three stars and a comment about the cat. Seemed fair.

Food Poisoning Diaries

The Street Meat Roulette

Five mystery skewers at 2am on Khao San Road. The odds were never in my favour.

Bangkok, Thailand7 min read

The Universal Law of Street Meat

Here's something nobody tells you about street food in Bangkok. The quality is inversely proportional to how drunk you are when you buy it. At 2pm, sober and cautious, you'll dodge the mystery skewers like landmines. At 2am, six beers deep and convinced you're invincible, that same cart looks like a Michelin-starred operation.

I discovered this law the hard way on Khao San Road.

How It Started

The night was going brilliantly. I'd met some Swedish backpackers at a hostel bar, and we'd been working our way through a bucket of something that tasted like petrol mixed with Red Bull. Someone suggested food. Someone else pointed at a cart with meat on sticks.

The vendor was this tiny bloke with approximately four teeth and a grill that looked like it had survived the Vietnam War. Smoke was pouring off it. The meat was... brown. Could've been chicken. Could've been pork. Could've been the neighbor's cat. I didn't ask questions.

Three skewers for 60 baht. Absolute bargain. Tasted like charcoal and fish sauce and victory. I ate five.

The Swedes went for pad thai from a proper stall with actual lighting. They're probably still alive and thriving. I went for street meat roulette and lost spectacularly.

The Morning After Everything Went Wrong

I woke up at 6am convinced my intestines were trying to escape through my mouth. Then the other end joined the rebellion. I won't describe the next two hours in detail because you're probably eating, but imagine both ends of a firehose operating simultaneously while you question every decision you've ever made.

My hostel roommate, a German engineer named Klaus, took one look at me and said "Street meat?" like he'd seen this tragedy play out a hundred times before. He probably had.

I needed medicine. I needed it desperately. But I was in Thailand, I spoke about seven words of Thai, and I was too weak to even google translate properly. Klaus was already gone for the day, the selfish bastard.

The Pharmacy Mime Olympics

The pharmacy was three blocks away. Those three blocks felt like crossing the Sahara. I shuffled in looking like a ghost who'd been hit by a bus, and the pharmacist, this lovely woman who spoke zero English, just smiled at me.

What followed was the most degrading game of charades I've ever played. I pointed at my stomach. She offered me antacids. I shook my head and made a... how do I put this... explosive gesture with my hands. She looked confused. I tried again, this time with sound effects.

The other customers were staring. A kid started crying. I didn't care anymore. Dignity had left the building around the same time as my third toilet sprint of the morning.

Finally, FINALLY, she understood. She handed me a box of something with a picture that roughly matched my situation. I paid 200 baht without checking the price. Would've paid 2000. She also gave me these rehydration sachets that tasted like salty sadness but probably saved my life.

The Long Road Back to Solid Food

I spent the next 48 hours in my bunk, sipping water and eating plain rice like some kind of medieval peasant. My food poisoning diaries were filling up faster than I'd expected when I started this trip.

Klaus came back each evening to check if I was still alive. On day two, he brought me bananas and told me about the time he ate bad oysters in Vietnam and shit himself on a overnight bus. It was the most emotionally vulnerable moment we'd shared. Traveler bonding is weird.

I missed a boat trip to the floating markets. I missed a Muay Thai match. I missed two full days of my precious travel insurance covered vacation lying in a room that smelled like Tiger Balm and regret.

The worst part wasn't even the physical misery. It was lying there at 3am, staring at the ceiling, mentally calculating how much I'd spent on those skewers versus how much I was losing by being bedbound. Five mystery meat sticks had cost me about 400 bucks in missed experiences and hostel nights I couldn't get refunded. That's 80 dollars per skewer. I could've eaten at a actual restaurant with walls and health certificates.

Lessons You Learn On Your Back

When you're lying in a bunk bed in Bangkok, destroyed by street meat, you have a lot of time to think. Mostly you think about toilets and whether you'll ever eat solid food again. But sometimes you think about bigger things.

Like how traveling is just a series of stupid decisions that either turn into good stories or hospital visits. How confidence and alcohol make you believe you're immune to basic food safety. How a 60-baht dinner can humble you more effectively than any temple or meditation retreat.

I did eventually recover. Ate my first proper meal on day three, a plain omelet that tasted like angels singing. I stayed in Bangkok another week and ate street food again, because I'm an idiot, but at least I was a slightly more cautious idiot. I stuck to the carts with long queues and visible ingredients. Revolutionary strategy, I know.

That mystery meat cart is probably still there on Khao San Road, still operating at 2am, still destroying drunk backpackers who think they're invincible. The vendor with four teeth is probably a millionaire by now. And somewhere in my memory, filed under "Expensive Lessons," are those five skewers that taught me more about humility than any amount of temple-hopping ever could.
